# The Verhoeven Conservation Studio

The Verhoeven Conservation Studio is the in-house conservation department of the Verhoeven Museum of Northern Light. It was established in 1978 as part of the Verhoeven Annexe and handles the care, examination, and restoration of the museum's collection.

The studio specialises in the treatment of nineteenth-century oil paintings, with particular expertise in the thin glaze techniques characteristic of the Aalderen School. It also maintains the museum's environmental monitoring programme.

A significant project undertaken by the studio was the 2004 cleaning of van Loon's "Grey Sea, Low Tide," which removed a discoloured varnish layer and revealed cooler tones in the sky that had been obscured for decades. The treatment was documented in the museum's internal conservation records.

The studio is led by a head conservator supported by two assistant conservators and a rotating programme of postgraduate interns. It does not undertake commercial work for external clients and treats only works in the museum's own collection or works on long-term loan.

The studio's technical examination facilities include infrared reflectography and X-radiography, used to study underdrawings and changes in composition beneath the paint surface.

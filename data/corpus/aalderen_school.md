# The Aalderen School

The Aalderen School is a fictional movement of Dutch landscape, marine, and interior painters active between approximately 1840 and 1920. The movement takes its name from the northern town of Aalderen, where many of its members lived and worked.

The School is characterised by a preoccupation with low, diffuse northern daylight, muted tonal palettes, and quiet, unpeopled or sparsely peopled scenes. Its painters generally avoided dramatic contrast and bright colour, favouring subtle gradations of grey, ochre, and blue.

The movement is usually divided into two generations. The first generation, founded by Pieter van Loon, focused on marine and coastal subjects. The second generation, led by Margriet Dekker, turned increasingly to domestic interiors.

The Aalderen School had no formal manifesto and was never an organised society. The grouping was applied retrospectively by the critic Willem Aarts in a 1924 essay, "The Painters of the Grey Light," which first defined the movement as a coherent tradition.

The largest single collection of Aalderen School works is held by the Verhoeven Museum of Northern Light. Smaller holdings exist in various fictional regional collections, but the Verhoeven is regarded as the definitive institution for the study of the movement.
